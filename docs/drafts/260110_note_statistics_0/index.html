Introduction

Biologists design experiments everyday, but sometimes they don’t really think about experiment design.

The basic principles of experiment design are listed below:

  1. Questions/goal of the experiment
  2. Comparison/control
  3. Replication
  4. Randomization
  5. Stratification/blocking
  6. Factorial experiments

Experimental Design

Let’s imagine a simple scenario:

Question: Does salted drinking water affect blood pressure (BP) in mice?

Experiment:

  1. Provide a mouse with water containing 1% NaCl.
  2. Wait 14 days.
  3. Measure BP.

Comparison/Control

You need a reference group to see the effect of salted drinking water.

Common control group designs could be:

  • Compare BP in mice fed salt water to BP in mice fed plain water.
  • Compare BP in strain A mice fed salt water to BP in strain B mice fed salt water.

Ideally, the experimental group is compared to concurrent controls (rather than to historical controls).

Replication

Replicates are needed to 1) reduce the effect of uncontrolled variation (increase precision), and to 2) quantify uncertainty.

In this scenario, 10 mouse per group would be a reasonable design.

An estimate is of no value without some statement of the uncertainty in the estimate.

Randomization

Experimental subjects (‘units’) should be assigned to treatment groups at random to 1) avoid bias and 2) to control the role of chance.

At random does NOT mean haphazardly.

It also allows the later use of probability theory, thus giving a solid foundation for statistical analysis.

Stratification/Blocking

Suppose that some BP measurements will be made in the morning, and some in the afternoon. If you anticipate a difference between morning and the afternoon - ensure that within each period, there are equal numbers of subjects in each treatment group (this is called stratification, or blocking); also, take account of the difference between periods in your analysis.

Let’s imagine we have 6 F mice, 6 M mice; half to be treated (T), half left untreated (C); we can only work with 6 mice per day. How to assign individuals to treatment groups, and to days?

  • Bad Design:

    D1 D2
    F-T F-C M-T M-C
    F-T F-C M-T M-C
    F-T F-C M-T M-C

  • Randomized Design:

    D1 D2
    M-T F-C M-T F-C
    F-T F-T M-C M-C
    F-T M-T F-C M-C

  • Stratified Design:

    D1 D2
    M-C M-C M-T F-C
    M-T F-T F-C M-T
    F-C F-T M-C F-T
Principles of Randomization and Stratification
  • If you can (and want to), fix a variable. (e.g., only use 8-week old M mice from a single strain.)
  • If you don’t fix a variable, stratify it. (e.g., use 8 week and 12 week old M mice, and stratify them respect to age.)
  • If you can neither fix nor stratify a variable, randomize it.

Factorial Experiments

Suppose we are interested in the effect of both salt water and a high-fat diet on blood pressure.

Ideally, we will look at all 4 treatments in 1 experiment; thus, we can 1) learn more (=is there any interactive outcome?). 2) make it more efficient compared to doing all single-factor experiments.

Other Points to Consider

  • Blinding: measurements made by people can be influenced by unconscious biases. Ideally, dissections and measurements should be made without knowledge of the treatment applied.

  • Internal controls: it can be useful to use the subjects themselves as their own controls (e.g., consider the response after vs. before treatment) to increase precision.

  • Representativeness: are the subjects/tissues you’re studying really representative of the population you want to study? Ideally, your study material is a stratified random sample from the population of interest.

  • Power: Experimental Design considerations (balanced designs) and sample size calculations are crucial, in particular w/ regards to your predictor of interest.
